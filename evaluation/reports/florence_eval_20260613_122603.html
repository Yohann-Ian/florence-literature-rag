Is duty in the Aeneid something Aeneas chooses, or something imposed on him? And does that distinction matter?

Florence

Duty and Will in the Aeneid

The question of whether Aeneas chooses his duty or merely submits to it strikes at the heart of Virgil's tragic vision—and the passages before us, though fragmentary, illuminate the tension without resolving it.

The Weight of Imposition

Consider the moment in Source 1: Aeneas departs the Sibyl's cave "Sad... and full of discontent, / Unknowing whom the sacred Sibyl meant." This is not the posture of a man who has freely embraced his mission. He walks in grief, puzzling over cryptic divine commands with Achates, his companion "with equal cares oppress'd." The duty here arrives as oracle—obscure, demanding, non-negotiable. Aeneas does not deliberate whether to "pay first his pious dues"; he simply goes, bearing the weight of instructions he does not fully understand.

This is pietas as burden, not as choice. The Latin concept encompasses duty to gods, family, and fate simultaneously, and Virgil consistently shows Aeneas staggering under its demands rather than striding forward in confident purpose.

Yet Something Like Assent

And yet—Ilioneus's speech in Source 2 complicates this picture. Describing Aeneas to Dido, he calls him "Observant of the right, religious of his word." These are not descriptions of mere compliance; they suggest character, disposition, a man whose nature aligns with obligation. The phrase "religious of his word" implies that Aeneas has internalised duty as identity. He is not simply commanded to be faithful; he is faithful.

Moreover, we learn that after Hector's death, Aeneas was chosen by Misenus—"and he chose as well." This small detail matters: even within a world of fate and divine machinery, Virgil preserves moments of mutual election, of wills meeting.

Does the Distinction Matter?

Here I think we must be honest: Virgil refuses to let us off easily. The Aeneid is not a Stoic tract celebrating joyful acceptance of fate, nor is it a proto-existentialist drama of radical freedom. It occupies the more uncomfortable middle ground where duty is both imposed and requires continuous, costly assent.

The distinction matters precisely because Virgil makes us feel it mattering to Aeneas. His sadness, his discontent, his grief—these are not failures of piety but the price of piety. A duty that cost nothing to accept would not be duty at all, merely preference. What makes Aeneas's pietas significant is that he continues toward Italy despite the sorrow, not in its absence.

The tragedy is that Aeneas cannot simply choose his duty freely (it comes from the gods, from Troy's ashes, from destiny), nor can he simply be compelled (the poem would lose its moral weight if he were merely a puppet). He must make his own what was never his to refuse. This is perhaps the most psychologically honest account of obligation in ancient literature—and one that resonates uncomfortably with anyone who has ever fulfilled a responsibility they did not ask for but could not honourably abandon.

Duty in the Aeneid is largely imposed — by the gods, by fate, by the weight of his people's survival. Aeneas does not want to leave Dido. He does not want to descend to the underworld. He does not want war. But pietas — his defining virtue — demands that personal desire yield to collective obligation. Whether a duty chosen and a duty imposed produce the same moral weight is one of the poem's unresolved questions. Virgil seems to suggest that whether duty is chosen or imposed makes no difference to what history demands of you — Rome gets built either way — but makes every difference to what it costs the man who builds it. The distinction matters enormously if you ask what kind of moral universe the poem inhabits. A hero who chooses duty freely is admirable. A hero who submits to duty he did not choose and would not choose is something more ambiguous and more human. The gods in the Aeneid are not benevolent — they are instruments of historical necessity. Jupiter does not ask Aeneas whether he consents to found Rome. Juno does not ask Dido whether she consents to be destroyed by it. The poem's tragedy is that pietas — doing what is right — and happiness are structurally incompatible. Aeneas cannot have both Rome and Dido. The poem does not pretend he can. It simply shows him choosing Rome, again and again, at a cost Virgil counts carefully.

Did Milton write Paradise Lost to challenge the religious and political establishment of his time?

Florence

Liberty, Tyranny, and the Architecture of Obedience in Paradise Lost

Milton's Paradise Lost presents one of the most intellectually demanding treatments of authority and rebellion in English literature—demanding precisely because it refuses the easy consolations of either absolute submission or romantic defiance. The passages before us reveal a poet wrestling with questions that were, for him, matters of life and death: having defended regicide and served Cromwell's republic, Milton wrote this epic blind and politically defeated, under a restored monarchy that had executed some of his colleagues. The poem's treatment of tyranny and liberty cannot be disentangled from this history.

The Paradox of Satanic Merit

Source 2 offers Satan enthroned in Hell, "by merit raised / To that bad eminence"—a phrase whose bitter irony cuts in multiple directions. Satan claims legitimacy through a combination of "just right, and the fixed laws of Heaven" and "free choice / With what besides in council or in fight / Hath been achieved of merit." He presents himself as a constitutional monarch whose authority rests on both divine ordination and popular consent, "Yielded with full consent."

This is, of course, a parody of legitimate rule—but it is a knowing parody. Milton, who had argued strenuously for the people's right to depose tyrants, here shows Satan deploying the very language of republican legitimacy. The effect is unsettling. Are we to conclude that such arguments are inherently Satanic? Or rather that Satan, the great counterfeiter, can mimic any political vocabulary while hollowing out its substance?

I would argue the latter. Satan's "merit" is real in one sense—he did rise through ambition and force—but it serves only "Vain war with Heaven," an enterprise "by success untaught." His throne is "safe, unenvied" precisely because it rules over ruin. The passage exposes not the illegitimacy of merit-based authority per se, but the corruption that occurs when ambition becomes untethered from reason and gratitude.

Satan's Self-Diagnosis

Source 3 provides the extraordinary moment when Satan, alone, speaks with devastating honesty about his own fall. "He deserved no such return / From me, whom he created what I was / In that bright eminence, and with his good / Upbraided none; nor was his service hard."

This is not the voice of a rebel against tyranny. Satan explicitly acknowledges that God's rule was not oppressive—"What could be less than to afford him praise, / The easiest recompence"—and that his rebellion stemmed from pride rather than grievance. The psychological acuity here is remarkable: "lifted up so high / I 'sdeined subjection, and thought one step higher / Would set me highest."

Milton presents rebellion not as a response to injustice but as a pathology of the will. Satan's tragedy lies in his inability to understand that "a grateful mind / By owing owes not, but still pays, at once / Indebted and discharged." Gratitude, properly understood, is not servitude but a form of freedom—the recognition of relationship that does not diminish the self. Satan experiences obligation as "burdensome," as debt that can never be discharged, because his pride prevents him from receiving grace as gift.

The political implications are significant. Milton is not arguing that all rebellion is Satanic—he himself had rebelled against Charles I. Rather, he distinguishes between rebellion against genuine tyranny (which he continued to defend) and rebellion against legitimate authority motivated by pride. The difference lies not in the act but in the moral condition of the rebel.

Michael's Theory of Political Servitude

Source 1 presents the most explicitly political theology in these passages. Michael's speech to Adam articulates a theory of tyranny that is genuinely radical in its implications—though not in the direction modern readers might expect.

"True liberty," Michael declares, "Is lost, which always with right reason dwells / Twinned, and from her hath no dividual being." Liberty and reason are not merely allied but ontologically inseparable. When "Reason in man obscured, or not obeyed," the result is that "upstart passions catch the government / From reason; and to servitude reduce / Man, till then free."

This is the crucial move: internal tyranny precedes and enables external tyranny. "Therefore, since he permits / Within himself unworthy powers to reign / Over free reason, God, in judgement just, / Subjects him from without to violent lords."

The political theology here is severe. Tyranny is both punishment and consequence—nations "decline so low / From virtue, which is reason" that their subjection becomes "no wrong, / But justice." Yet Milton immediately adds: "Though to the tyrant thereby no excuse." The tyrant remains culpable even when serving as God's instrument of judgement.

This is not a comfortable doctrine for any political faction. It condemns the tyrannised for their moral failures while simultaneously condemning the tyrant. It suggests that political liberation requires moral reformation—that external freedom without internal liberty is unstable or illusory. For a defeated republican writing under the Restoration, this may have been the only intellectually honest position available: the English people had chosen to restore the monarchy, and Milton must account for that choice without abandoning his principles.

The Dangerous Implications

What makes Milton genuinely dangerous—and why Paradise Lost continues to disturb—is that his argument cuts against the grain of modern political assumptions. We prefer to believe that liberty is a right independent of moral condition, that people deserve freedom regardless of their virtue. Milton disagrees. For him, a people who have surrendered reason to passion have already enslaved themselves; the external tyrant merely formalises an existing condition.

This is not a defence of tyranny—Milton's hatred of tyrants is evident throughout his work. But it is a rejection of the idea that political arrangements alone can secure freedom. The "Rational liberty" Michael describes requires the continuous exercise of reason over passion, gratitude over pride, self-governance over self-indulgence.

Satan, in this reading, is not a freedom fighter but the archetype of false liberty—one who mistakes licence for freedom, pride for dignity, and rebellion for independence. His tragedy is that he could have been free, had he understood that service to a good God is not servitude but the highest expression of rational nature.

Raskolnikov's Theory and Its Unravelling

The passages provided offer Nabokov's critical perspective on Crime and Punishment rather than direct textual evidence from Dostoevsky's novel itself—a distinction worth noting, as Nabokov was famously hostile to Dostoevsky's work, calling it "moral and artistic stupidity." Nevertheless, his critique illuminates something important about how the novel treats Raskolnikov's theory of extraordinary men.

According to Nabokov's reading, Raskolnikov's motivation for murder is "extremely muddled"—and this muddiness may be precisely Dostoevsky's point. The theory of the extraordinary man who may "transgress moral law" cannot survive contact with the actual psychology of transgression. Nabokov identifies what he sees as a poorly executed transition: Raskolnikov moves from "aspiring benefactor of the world toward an aspiring tyrant for the sake of his own power." Whether or not Dostoevsky handles this transition with sufficient craft (Nabokov thinks not), the trajectory itself constitutes an argument. The Napoleonic theory collapses into mere will-to-power; the philosophical justification reveals itself as rationalisation.

What Dostoevsky appears to propose as the novel's counter-argument is suffering—specifically, "suffering openly accepted, the deliberate self-abasement and humiliation before his fellow-humans." The extraordinary man theory posits that certain individuals stand above ordinary moral constraints; Dostoevsky's response is that redemption requires the opposite movement—radical submission to human community and its judgments.

Nabokov's scepticism ("whether he will kill again is impossible to say") points to a genuine tension the novel may not fully resolve. Does Raskolnikov's eventual confession represent genuine moral transformation, or merely psychological collapse? The text, as Nabokov reads it, leaves this ambiguous.

The Ontological Violence of O'Brien's Interrogation

The passages from Nineteen Eighty-Four present one of literature's most disturbing portraits of totalitarian power—not merely as political domination, but as an assault on the very conditions of human existence and knowledge.

The Annihilation of the Subject

O'Brien's declaration "You do not exist" (Source 1) is not rhetorical flourish but doctrinal precision. The Party's project, as these passages reveal, is nothing less than the dissolution of individual consciousness as an independent locus of truth. When O'Brien tells Winston that Big Brother "is the embodiment of the Party," he establishes a hierarchy of reality in which collective power precedes and supersedes individual being. Winston's existence becomes contingent, provisional—real only insofar as the Party acknowledges it.

This is reinforced by the extraordinary claim in Source 5: "Reality is inside the skull... We control matter because we control the mind." O'Brien dismisses "nineteenth-century ideas about the laws of Nature" with contempt. The Party's ambition is not to describe reality but to constitute it. This represents a perverse inversion of Enlightenment epistemology—rather than the mind conforming to the world, the world must conform to the Party's mind.

The Mechanics of Memory Destruction

Sources 2, 3, and 4 document the systematic destruction of Winston's capacity for historical memory. The technique is precise: O'Brien does not simply demand that Winston say Oceania has always been at war with Eastasia—he demands Winston genuinely remember it thus. The "devastating explosion" in Source 4, which leaves "a large patch of emptiness, as though a piece had been taken out of his brain," literalises what doublethink accomplishes psychologically: the surgical removal of inconvenient truth.

What makes this particularly chilling is O'Brien's framing of Winston's accurate memories as pathology: "You are mentally deranged. You suffer from a defective memory" (Source 2). The Party medicalises dissent, recasting fidelity to truth as illness requiring cure. Winston's "small effort of the will" that he failed to make is, of course, the effort to stop remembering—to embrace the cognitive self-mutilation the Party demands.

The Question of Julia

O'Brien's treatment of Julia's "conversion" (Source 1) deserves attention. His language—"All her rebelliousness, her deceit, her folly, her dirty-mindedness—everything has been burned out of her"—presents the destruction of personality as purification. The word "perfect" applied to her conversion suggests an aesthetic satisfaction in the annihilation of human complexity. When Winston asks "You tortured her?" O'Brien's non-answer is itself an answer: the question is beneath consideration, irrelevant to the Party's moral framework in which torture is simply technique.

Power Without Limit or Purpose

Perhaps most striking is the Party's frank admission that its power serves no external end. O'Brien does not claim to be building utopia or serving historical necessity. The levitation he mentions in Source 5—"I could float off this floor like a soap bubble if I wish to"—is declined not because it is impossible but because "the Party does not wish it." Power here becomes entirely self-referential, constrained only by its own will. This distinguishes Orwell's vision from earlier dystopian or totalitarian ideologies that at least gestured toward human flourishing as justification.

Florence

Pleasure and Pain as Instruments of Power

The question of which model proves "more effective" depends entirely on what we mean by effectiveness—and here the two novels diverge not merely in method but in fundamental purpose.

Two Philosophies of Control

Mustapha Mond's confession in the Huxley passage reveals a regime that has made a calculated exchange: truth and beauty sacrificed at the altar of happiness. The World State's genius lies in making its citizens complicit in their own subjugation. "People were ready to have even their appetites controlled," Mond observes—the population chose comfort after the trauma of the Nine Years' War. Control through pleasure requires no enforcement apparatus because the controlled do not experience themselves as controlled. Bernard and Helmholtz are not tortured; they are exiled. The system can afford magnanimity because it faces no genuine threat.

O'Brien's vision in the Orwell passage is something altogether different—and, I would argue, more philosophically honest about the nature of power. "Unless he is suffering, how can you be sure that he is obeying your will and not his own?" This is not merely sadism (though it is that); it is a recognition that pleasure-based control contains an irreducible ambiguity. The happy subject might be obeying, or might simply be pursuing their own gratification, which happens to align with the state's wishes. Pain eliminates this uncertainty. The Party wants not compliance but submission—the conscious knowledge of powerlessness.

The Question of Effectiveness

If effectiveness means stability with minimal expenditure of resources, Huxley's model is clearly superior. The World State runs almost frictionlessly; its dissidents are few and easily managed. Orwell's Oceania requires constant surveillance, perpetual war, and an elaborate apparatus of torture—tremendous ongoing costs.

But if effectiveness means total power, O'Brien would dismiss Huxley's World Controllers as amateurs who have settled for something less than dominion. They have created contented cattle, not broken wills. The Party's project is more ambitious and more terrible: not to make rebellion impossible by making it unthinkable, but to make the rebel love his own destruction. "The old civilizations claimed that they were founded on love or justice. Ours is founded upon hatred."

A Darker Observation

There is something uncomfortably prescient in Huxley's model for our present moment—the soft totalitarianism of algorithmic pleasure, the voluntary surrender of attention and autonomy for entertainment. We are not tortured into compliance; we are amused into it. Orwell's nightmare requires villains; Huxley's requires only consumers.

Yet Orwell understood something Huxley perhaps did not fully reckon with: that some forms of power are not for anything beyond themselves. The Party does not want a quiet life. It wants, as O'Brien says, "a boot stamping on a human face—forever." This is power as metaphysical project, not social engineering.

A system that can look at the elimination of human depth and call it progress is more frightening than one that simply brutalises, because at least brutalisation can be recognised and named.

Both Orwell and Huxley imagined totalitarian futures. What is the fundamental difference in how each writer thinks control works?

Florence

Control Through Pain Versus Control Through Pleasure

The fundamental difference lies in what each writer identifies as the mechanism of subjugation—and, by extension, what they believe makes human beings controllable.

Orwell's vision is one of coercive negation. In the Ministry of Love, O'Brien does not merely want Winston to comply; he wants to annihilate Winston's capacity for independent reality-testing. The exchange about the past's existence is revealing: O'Brien presses Winston on whether the past exists "concretely, in space" or only "in records" and "in human memories." The implication is chilling—if the past exists only in minds and documents, and the Party controls both, then the Party controls reality itself. Winston's "deadly helplessness" comes not from physical pain alone but from the epistemological vertigo of doublethink: the possibility that O'Brien has "really forgotten" the photograph, has "forgotten his denial of remembering it, and forgotten the act of forgetting." Control in Orwell operates through the systematic destruction of the mind's ability to know anything independently of power. The Party does not need you to be happy; it needs you to be empty.

Huxley, by contrast—though not directly quoted in these passages—imagined control through satiation rather than deprivation. Where Orwell's Party starves and tortures, Huxley's World State floods citizens with pleasure, distraction, and pharmacological contentment. The threat is not the boot stamping on a human face, but the face too blissed-out to notice it has been stamped upon.

The distinction is philosophically profound: Orwell feared we would be destroyed by what we hate; Huxley feared we would be destroyed by what we love. Orwell's totalitarianism requires constant violence against truth; Huxley's requires only that truth become irrelevant—drowned in a sea of trivial satisfactions.

What the Orwell passages demonstrate with particular force is the intimacy of his terror. O'Brien approaches Winston with "the air of a teacher taking pains with a wayward but promising child." This is not distant bureaucratic oppression but something almost pastoral—a patient, methodical remaking of the soul. The horror is that the Party wants not just obedience but love.

Reference

Orwell believes control works through fear, pain, and the destruction of truth — the Party rules by making reality itself malleable and resistance physically unbearable. Huxley believes control works through satisfaction — give people enough pleasure and they will surrender freedom without noticing. Orwell's citizens know they are oppressed and are broken anyway. Huxley's citizens do not know they are oppressed because oppression has been made comfortable. The two dystopias are mirror images of the same insight: that human freedom is fragile and can be destroyed from either direction. Neil Postman, writing decades after both novels, argued that Huxley was right and Orwell was wrong — that the threat to free culture would come not from external censorship but from the irrelevance that follows when everything is entertainment. That argument has only gained force. But it misses something Orwell understood that Huxley did not — that pain and fear are still the instruments of control in the places where control is most total. Huxley's model requires a population that has already been pacified. Orwell's model shows what happens before pacification is complete, when there are still people who remember that things were different. The two novels are not competing predictions about the same future. They are descriptions of two different stages of the same process — Orwell shows the conquest, Huxley shows the occupation.

Yet note the crucial detail: "The rest stood trembling, struck with awe divine; / Aeneas only, conscious to the sign, / Presag'd th' event." Providence here is legible only to its chosen instrument. The Trojans tremble; Aeneas alone interprets. This is not universal revelation but private commission, and it isolates the hero even as it empowers him.

Venus herself, described as one "whose indulgent care / Foresaw the dangers of the growing war," operates through foreknowledge—she sees what will come and prepares her son accordingly. The arms are "Fated from force of steel," their invulnerability itself decreed. Aeneas can declare with confidence, "Heav'n calls me to the war," because the semiotic system of omens functions reliably in his world. The gods speak; the pious hero reads.

Providence as Wound: The Miltonic Counterpoint

Milton's treatment in Source 4 offers a devastating inversion. Where Virgil's heaven sends arms and assurance, Milton's heaven responds to human choice with cosmic grief: "Earth trembled from her entrails, as again / In pangs; and Nature gave a second groan; / Sky loured; and, muttering thunder, some sad drops / Wept at completing of the mortal sin." The thunder that in Virgil confirms divine favour here marks divine sorrow. Nature itself becomes a mourner at the Fall.

The passage is remarkable for what it reveals about the structure of Miltonic providence. Adam eats "Against his better knowledge; not deceived, / But fondly overcome with female charm." This is not ignorance but wilful transgression—free will exercised toward catastrophe. Yet the poem refuses simple condemnation. Eve weeps "Tenderly" for joy that Adam "his love / Had so ennobled, as of choice to incur / Divine displeasure for her sake, or death." There is something genuinely moving here, even as Milton's parenthetical judgment—"for such compliance bad / Such recompence best merits"—insists on moral clarity. The narrative voice fractures: we are asked simultaneously to recognise the sin and to feel the love that occasions it.

The bitter irony of "They swim in mirth, and fancy that they feel / Divinity within them breeding wings" captures the tragic structure of the Fall. They believe themselves elevated precisely at the moment of their degradation. Providence in Milton does not prevent catastrophe; it permits it, then works through its consequences toward redemption. This is a harder theodicy than Virgil's—one that requires the full weight of human suffering as its instrument.

The Father's Grief: Where Providence Fails to Console

Source 2 presents perhaps the most emotionally complex moment in these passages: Aeneas's encounter with the slain Pallas and his grieving father Evander. Aeneas speaks with ceremonial dignity—"Accept whate'er Aeneas can afford"—promising that the youth's arms will remain "Inviolate, and sacred to the slain." He even offers a grim consolation: "There to thy fellow ghosts with glory tell: / 'Twas by the great Aeneas hand I fell.'" But this is precisely what Pallas cannot tell, for he fell not by Aeneas's hand but under Aeneas's command, in Aeneas's war.

The passage's true weight falls in its final image: "Meantime, his father, now no father, stood, / And wash'd his wounds by Tiber's yellow flood." That parenthetical—"now no father"—is devastating in its compression. Evander's identity has been annihilated by his son's death. He is "Oppress'd with anguish, panting, and o'erspent, / His fainting limbs against an oak he leant." Providence may guarantee Rome's founding, but it offers nothing to this broken man leaning against a tree by a yellow river.

This is where Virgil's sympathy strains against his poem's official ideology. The Aeneid must celebrate the fated empire; it cannot quite suppress the cost. Evander's grief stands as mute testimony against any triumphalist reading of Roman destiny.

Sacred Hunger: The Corruption Providence Permits

Source 3 introduces another dimension: the moral chaos that providence allows to flourish. The murder of Polydore by the "faithless tyrant" of Thrace prompts Aeneas's famous exclamation: "O sacred hunger of pernicious gold! / What bands of faith can impious lucre hold?" The adjective "sacred" (sacra in the Latin) carries its full ambiguity—both "accursed" and "holy," as though greed itself has become a dark divinity.

Priam sent his son "Loaded with gold... far / From noise and tumults, and destructive war," seeking safety. Instead, the gold became the instrument of murder. Providence here operates negatively: the prodigy of bleeding branches reveals the crime, prompting the Trojans to "leave that execrable shore." Divine communication functions, but only after the atrocity. The gods warn; they do not prevent.

Venus's Complaint: Providence Questioned

Source 5 offers the most direct challenge to providential order, voiced remarkably by a goddess herself. Venus confronts Jupiter (implicitly) with bitter questions: "How could my pious son thy pow'r incense? / Or what, alas! is vanish'd Troy's offence?" The epithet "pious"—Aeneas's defining characteristic—becomes an accusation. If piety cannot secure divine favour, what can?

Venus recalls Jupiter's promise: "a progeny divine / Of Romans, rising from the Trojan line, / In after times should hold the world in awe." She demands accountability: "How is your doom revers'd?" The passage reveals that even within the divine council, providence appears inconsistent, its workings opaque. "Then fates to fates I could oppose," Venus says, "but now, / When Fortune still pursues her former blow, / What can I hope?"

This is extraordinary. A goddess confesses uncertainty about the cosmic order. She contrasts Aeneas's suffering with Antenor's easy passage to found Padua—"Could pass secure"—implying that fate distributes its burdens arbitrarily. The question "What end of labours has your will decreed?" resonates beyond its immediate context. It is the question every character in both epics might ask.

Narrative Sympathy and Ideological Strain

What emerges from these passages is not a simple contrast between pagan fate and Christian providence, but a shared struggle with the moral implications of cosmic order. Both poets commit to teleological frameworks—Rome must be founded; humanity must fall and be redeemed—yet both allow their narratives to register the human cost with unsettling power.

Virgil's sympathy for the defeated, the grieving, the sacrificed runs as a counter-current to his celebration of empire. The father who is "now no father," the youth whose blood renews the lances that killed him, the goddess who questions fate itself—these figures complicate any reading of the Aeneid as straightforward imperial propaganda. Milton, similarly, cannot prevent our sympathy from flowing toward Eve's tenderness, Adam's tragic loyalty, even as the poem insists on their culpability.

Perhaps this is what distinguishes great theodicy from mere apologetics: the willingness to let the wound remain visible, to show providence achieving its ends through means that the human heart cannot wholly accept. Both poets understand that to make divine purpose fully palatable would be to falsify human experience. The glory of Rome rises from Evander's grief; the redemption of humanity begins in Nature's groan. These are not contradictions to be resolved but tensions to be inhabited.

Chichikov and the Limits of Forster's Framework

Forster's distinction between flat and round characters rests on a deceptively simple criterion: the flat character "can be expressed in one sentence" and possesses no existence outside that defining quality, while the round character surprises us convincingly, containing multitudes that resist reduction. Mrs Micawber's "I never will desert Mr. Micawber" exhausts her; she is that sentence. The round character, by contrast, cannot be so pinned—Forster cites "all the principal characters in War and Peace, all the Dostoevsky characters" as exemplars of this irreducible complexity.

Yet when we turn to Nabokov's reading of Chichikov in Dead Souls, we encounter a figure who troubles this binary in productive ways. Nabokov describes Chichikov's "aura" as "continued and symbolized" by his possessions—the silver snuffbox with its violets, the travelling case, the eau de cologne rubbed on his "sub-human, obscene body, as white and as plump as that of some fat woodboring larva." These objects do not merely accessorise Chichikov; they expand him "in a kind of spherical way to the remotest regions of the book." The language here—"spherical," suggesting roundness—seems to align with Forster's category. And indeed, Chichikov possesses what Forster would recognise as interiority: a "hidden past" in smuggling, private rituals of self-perfuming, an anxiety about the "miserable reek of inferno" that permeates him.

But Nabokov's crucial phrase complicates matters: Chichikov is "a fake and a phantom clothed in a pseudo-Pickwickian rotundity of flesh." The roundness is pseudo—a performance, a disguise. His complexity is not the organic complexity of a Natasha Rostova discovering herself through experience; it is the complexity of concealment, of surfaces layered over an essential hollowness. The snuffbox's violets mask something rotten; the cologne smothers rather than expresses. Chichikov has depth, but it is the depth of an abyss rather than a soul.

This suggests that Forster's framework, while useful, may be insufficiently attentive to a third category: the character who simulates roundness while remaining, at some fundamental level, opaque or void. Chichikov inverts this: he appears to have depth, yet that depth may itself be a kind of flatness, a single organising principle (acquisition, fraud, self-concealment) elaborately disguised.

The framework holds, then, but only if we recognise that Gogol—and Nabokov reading Gogol—is doing something the English novel tradition Forster draws upon rarely attempts: creating a character whose very being is counterfeit, whose roundness is a grotesque mask over what Nabokov calls the "phantom."
